Literature

  • Set Texts: Plot summaries, key events, characters, key quotes
  • Themes: Love, conflict, power, identity, betrayal, ambition
  • Character Analysis: Traits, motivations, development, relationships, key quotes
  • Context: Author background, historical/social context, literary movements, influences
  • Literary Devices:
    • Metaphor, simile, personification, imagery, symbolism, irony, allusion, foreshadowing, hyperbole, repetition
  • Analysis Techniques: PEEL (Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link), close reading, inference, identifying tone/mood
  • Worked Examples:
    • “Romeo and Juliet” – “star-crossed lovers” (metaphor), theme of love vs family loyalty
    • “Macbeth” – “vaulting ambition” (metaphor), theme of ambition and consequence
    • “Of Mice and Men” – “dreams vs reality”, Steinbeck uses repetition and imagery to highlight loneliness
  • Tips: Annotate text, memorise quotes, connect devices to theme, practise timed analysis

Language

  • Grammar: Parts of speech, verb tenses, subject-verb agreement, clauses, sentence types
  • Punctuation: Full stops, commas, semicolons, colons, dashes, brackets, apostrophes, quotation marks
  • Sentence Structure: Simple, compound, complex, compound-complex; clauses vs phrases; active vs passive voice
  • Writing Styles: Narrative, descriptive, persuasive, analytical, formal vs informal; planning structure for essays
  • Comprehension Skills: Skimming, scanning, inference, summarising, identifying tone and attitude, vocabulary context clues
  • Common Mistakes: Homophones (their/there/they’re), punctuation errors, run-on sentences, fragments, spelling pitfalls
  • Mini Grammar & Punctuation Table:
    AspectRuleExample
    CommaSeparate clauses, lists, introductory phrasesI bought apples, oranges, and bananas.
    SemicolonLink independent clausesI love tea; she prefers coffee.
    ColonIntroduce a list or explanationHe has three hobbies: reading, swimming, gaming.
    Full StopEnd of sentenceThe cat slept.
    ApostropheShow possession or contractionsTom's book, it's raining
  • Worked Examples:
    • Change to past perfect: “I eat breakfast” → “I had eaten breakfast”
    • Combine clauses: “I went home. I was tired.” → “I went home because I was tired.”
  • Tips: Plan writing, use varied sentence structures, proofread, check punctuation and grammar

Poetry

  • Literary Devices: Alliteration, assonance, rhyme, rhythm, enjambment, imagery, repetition, metaphor, personification, simile, symbolism
  • Form & Structure: Sonnet, free verse, ballad, limerick; stanza types; line length; caesura
  • Analysis Techniques: Identify theme, tone, mood, literary devices; connect to context
  • Comparing Poems: Compare theme, tone, devices, context
  • Worked Examples:
    • “The Tyger” – repetition and rhyme convey awe and fear
    • “Daffodils” – imagery and personification highlight beauty of nature
    • Compare: “The Tyger” vs “The Lamb” – contrasting tone, imagery, and symbolism
  • Tips: Annotate poems, highlight devices, memorise key quotes, practise timed comparisons

Writing & Exam Tips

  • Use PEEL (Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link) or TEEL structure for paragraphs
  • Plan essays: introduction, 2–3 main points, conclusion
  • Support points with textual evidence, quotes, or examples
  • Vary vocabulary & sentence structures, avoid repetition
  • Time management: allocate minutes per question, leave 5–10 min for checking
  • Practice past papers & model answers
  • Proofread for grammar, punctuation, spelling, and clarity
  • Highlight key themes and literary devices in the question before writing
  • Annotate text while reading for comprehension and analysis questions
  • Include context for authors, historical period, or literary movement where relevant
